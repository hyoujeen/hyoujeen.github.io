Literature & Media

"In my restless dreams, I see that town..."

In my restless dreams, I see that town. Silent Hill.
-Mary Shepherd-Sunderland from Silent Hill 2
Fog everywhere. Fog up the river, where it flows among green aits and meadows; fog down the river, where it rolls defiled among the tiers of shipping, and the waterside pollutions of a great (and dirty) city. Fog on the Essex marshes, fog on the Kentish heights. [...] Chance people on the bridges peeping over the parapets into a nether sky of fog, with fog all around them, as if they were up in a balloon, and hanging in the misty clouds.
-Charles Dickens' Bleak House

What once was a feature used to compensate for technical limitations now serves as an iconic part of the Silent Hill franchise. And after playing through the recently released Silent Hill 2 Remake, walking through the oppressive fog that envelops the desolate town embodying the protagonist's warped psyche, I couldn't help but be reminded of the beginning of Charles Dickens' Bleak House. Fog here, fog there, "[f]og everywhere." Once again, oppressive.

Yet, the fog also has an illuminating purpose in both works. A paradox that calls to us fans of Gothic literature and the survival horror genre alike. The fog, ironically, invites us to walk through its smothering haze and confront what we seek to hide away and repress. Fears, injustices, and ugly truths.
